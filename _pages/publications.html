4. Chemomechanical model of sperm locomotion reveals two modes of swimming
C. Li, B. Chakrabarti, P. Castilla, A. Mahajan and D. Saintillan (2023)
Physical Review Fluids (Editor's suggestion)
DOI
The propulsion of mammalian spermatozoa relies on the spontaneous periodic oscillation of their flagella. These oscillations are driven internally by the coordinated action of ATP-powered dynein motors that exert sliding forces between microtubule doublets, resulting in bending waves that propagate along the flagellum and enable locomotion. We present an integrated chemomechanical model of a freely swimming spermatozoon that uses a sliding-control model of the axoneme capturing the two-way feedback between motor kinetics and elastic deformations while accounting for detailed fluid mechanics around the moving cell. Nonlinear simulations are shown to produce spontaneous oscillations with realistic beating patterns and trajectories. Our results indicate that the swimming velocity does not vary monotonically with dynein activity, but instead displays two maxima corresponding to distinct modes of swimming, each characterized by qualitatively different wave forms and trajectories.

3. Euchromatin activity enhances segregation and compaction of heterochromatin in the cell nucleus
A. Mahajan, W. Yan, A. Zidovska, D. Saintillan and M. J. Shelley (2022)
Physical Review X
DOI
The fruit fly genome, like that of most higher organisms, is organized into active euchromatin and repressive heterochromatin compartments. Using a coarse-grained computational model that accounts for the activity of the transcriptional machinery on euchromatin and its hydrodynamic interactions, we show that euchromatin activity alone is sufficient to drive the segregation and compaction of heterochromatin. The active stresses and flows generated by euchromatin dynamics create an effective pressure that pushes heterochromatin towards the nuclear periphery, consistent with the conventional nuclear architecture observed in most cell types.

2. Self-induced hydrodynamic coil-stretch transition of active polymers
A. Mahajan and D. Saintillan (2022)
Physical Review E
DOI
We model an active polymer whose monomers exert active dipolar forces on the surrounding fluid, and study its dynamics using Brownian dynamics simulations with hydrodynamic interactions. The interplay of activity and hydrodynamic coupling between monomers leads to a rich conformational dynamics, including a self-induced coil-stretch transition as a function of activity. This transition, which arises from the flow generated by the polymer itself, bears similarities to the classic coil-stretch transition of passive polymers in extensional flows.
